The Fengdou System

A closed corporate token economy with a defining feature rarely studied in operations: two redemption channels with heterogeneous marginal cost to the firm.

⛏

Earn through work

Couriers accrue Fengdou by completing delivery jobs. Issuance volume is a firm-controlled lever — effectively the "money supply" of the economy.

🎮

Spend on virtual goods (cost = 0)

App skins, badges, status items. These deliver real utility to couriers yet cost the firm nothing at the margin.

📦

Spend on real goods (cost > 0)

Paper, food, daily necessities. Every redemption draws on the firm's annual budget from headquarters.

The bite: the budget must be spent close to its target. Overspend is infeasible; underspend triggers a budget cut the following year — a use-it-or-lose-it ratchet. The firm sets issuance and prices before observing how couriers will actually redeem, and it cannot dictate the virtual-vs-real split. It can only steer.

Research question

How should a firm design a token economy with dual-cost rewards to motivate labor while hitting a budget target it controls only indirectly?

This is not a cost-minimization problem and not a consumer loyalty program. Couriers earn the currency through effort; the firm faces a two-sided budget target; and one redemption channel is free. That combination — a labor-incentive scrip with heterogeneous marginal cost and an indirectly-controlled budget target — sits in a gap between the loyalty-program, gig-incentive, and scrip-monetary-policy literatures.

The central structural claim of the paper: the firm's three instruments map almost cleanly onto its three objectives — a near-separation result.

A Bilevel (Stackelberg) Model

The firm leads; couriers follow. Calibrated on the full SF transaction panel, then optimized normatively.

Lower level · couriers (followers)

A dynamic redemption-choice model

  • • Heterogeneous couriers earn tokens via effort each period.
  • • Each period they choose: virtual · real · hold/save (random-utility / threshold accumulation).
  • • Calibrated structurally on transaction-level redemptions, balances, and price variation.
Upper level · the firm (leader)

A dynamic policy-design program

  • • Choose issuance & price vectors to optimize the weighted three-goal objective.
  • • Budget targeting ≈ a newsvendor with two-sided penalty ⇒ critical-fractile policy.
  • • Float band ≈ Miller–Orr cash-management control limits.
  • • Multi-year ratchet ⇒ an MDP where today's underspend lowers tomorrow's budget state.

Why each analogy earns its place. The firm commits to issuance and prices before stochastic redemption demand realizes and is penalized for both over- and under-shoot — the newsvendor skeleton, with the virtual good acting as the substitution margin that thins the tails. The stock of outstanding Fengdou behaves like a money inventory, so keeping it in a band is a Miller–Orr problem. And because underspending permanently lowers next year's allocation, the leader's problem is genuinely dynamic — it may rationally inflate virtual-good attractiveness today to protect tomorrow's budget.

The Pressure-Valve Principle

The paper's signature idea: virtual goods are a free budget-steering valve. They absorb token demand at zero marginal cost, so their relative attractiveness — not issuance, not real prices — is the firm's sharpest instrument for hitting the budget target.

Illustrative. As virtual-good attractiveness λ rises, real-goods spend falls monotonically; the two-sided budget penalty is U-shaped with an interior optimum λ*.

Too attractive →

Couriers flock to free skins, real-goods spend undershoots, and the budget is ratcheted down next year.

Too unattractive →

Demand floods the real channel and spend overshoots the budget — infeasible.

The interior optimum λ*

Tunes the valve so realized real-goods spend lands on target — at zero incremental cost, while virtual goods still deliver incentive value. Issuance is then freed to do its real job: motivation.

Intended Contributions

Targeting a UTD-24 OR/OM outlet (Management Science / Operations Research).

01

A new problem class

Formalizes the dual-cost labor-incentive token economy with an indirectly-controlled, two-sided budget target — distinct from loyalty points, gig cash bonuses, and scrip systems.

02

The separation / pressure-valve theorem

Conditions under which issuance governs incentives while relative virtual/real pricing governs the budget — a clean decoupling of instruments and goals.

03

The ratchet distortion

Characterizes how the multi-year use-it-or-lose-it dynamic distorts the static optimum, and quantifies the welfare cost of budget myopia.

04

Calibrated policy counterfactuals

Using two years of SF field data: how much closer to budget, and how much more incentive value per yuan, an optimized policy would have delivered.

Positioning against the literature

• Loyalty / points programs — single currency, breakage, point pressure. We add heterogeneous marginal cost and earned-through-labor tokens.

• Gig-economy incentives — typically cash bonuses. Fengdou is non-cash scrip with a closed redemption market.

• Scrip monetary policy — hoarding/inflation in token economies. We add a firm budget target and a real-goods channel.

• Budget-ratchet effect — known in accounting; we embed it inside a prescriptive operations model.
