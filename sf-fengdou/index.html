The Decision Environment: Fengdou

Fengdou (丰豆) is SF Express's closed-loop reward token: couriers earn it through delivery work and redeem it through two channels with very different cost to the firm.

⛏

Earn through work

Tokens accrue per delivery job. Issuance volume is the "money supply" — a firm-controlled lever.

🎮

Virtual goods (cost = 0)

Skins, badges, status items — real utility to couriers, zero marginal cost to SF.

📦

Real goods (cost > 0)

Paper, food, daily necessities — every redemption draws on the annual budget.

Why this is hard to automate: the budget must be spent close to target — overspend is infeasible, and underspend triggers a budget cut next year (a use-it-or-lose-it ratchet). Levers are set before redemption is observed, and the firm cannot dictate the virtual-vs-real split. Much of what governs the right call — HQ politics, the ratchet, fairness — lives in the operators' heads, not the data. That is precisely where human judgment meets the algorithm.

The Human–AI Pipeline

The algorithm owns the statistical problem; the human owns the political-economy problem. We observe every step — including where they disagree.

Algorithm

1 · Forecast & optimize

Predicts courier redemption demand and the Fengdou float; recommends issuance & prices.

Human

2 · Adjust with private info

Operators override using signals the model lacks: ratchet risk, HQ pressure, campaigns, fairness.

Policy

3 · Commit final levers

The implemented issuance & price vector — which may differ from the recommendation.

Outcome

4 · Realized result

Budget adherence · incentive value · float stability — the score each override is judged against.

The data asset. Because both the algorithmic recommendation and the human-set final decision are recorded, we can isolate where and why operators deviate — and measure whether each deviation moved the firm closer to, or further from, its goals. Two deviation types matter: information deviations (the human knows more) and complexity / discretion deviations (the human won't or can't follow exactly).

Three Research Questions

From normative design to empirical effect to deployable system.

Q1

Optimal delegation (theory)

Which decisions should be delegated to the algorithm vs. escalated to a human who holds a private budget-ratchet signal? A normative decision-rights model.

Q2

Do overrides add value? (empirical)

Estimate the causal effect of operator overrides on budget adherence and incentive value — handling the endogeneity of when humans choose to intervene.

Q3

Collaborative system (prescriptive)

Design an adherence-aware recommendation policy that anticipates human deviation and routes the right calls to the right agent; quantify counterfactual gains.

The Decision Model Underneath

The collaboration runs on a dual-cost, budget-targeted token economy. Three firm levers map almost cleanly onto three goals — and the virtual-goods price is a free budget-steering valve.

Lever Primarily steers Mechanism
1 Token issuance Incentive power + float More tokens ⇒ stronger effort signal, larger outstanding liability.
2 Real-goods prices Budget burn rate Sets how fast real redemptions draw down the annual budget.
3 Virtual-goods prices Budget steering — the valve Diverts token demand toward the zero-cost channel at will.

Illustrative. As virtual-good attractiveness λ rises, real-goods spend falls; the two-sided budget penalty is U-shaped with an interior optimum λ*.

Pressure-valve principle

Virtual goods absorb token demand at zero cost, so their relative price — not issuance — is the sharpest budget-steering instrument. This is the structural insight the algorithm encodes.

Where the human enters

λ* depends on the ratchet and HQ stance — soft, forward-looking knowledge the operator holds privately. The optimal λ the algorithm computes is exactly what a well-informed human is positioned to correct.

Dynamics

Multi-period: token accumulation (Miller–Orr-style float band) and a budget ratchet making the firm's problem an MDP.

Intended Contributions

Targeting a UTD-24 OR/OM outlet (Management Science / Operations Research).

01

A new collaboration setting

First study of human–AI collaboration in a dual-cost, budget-targeted incentive economy, where the human's private signal is the budget ratchet itself.

02

An optimal-delegation model

Characterizes when to delegate to the algorithm vs. escalate to the human — an adherence-aware decision-rights policy with provable improvement over either alone.

03

Causal value of overrides

Using the recommendation-vs-override trace, identifies which operator deviations improve budget adherence and incentive value — and which merely add noise.

04

Calibrated policy counterfactuals

Quantifies how much closer to budget, and how much more incentive value per yuan, an optimally collaborative policy would have delivered over two years of SF data.

Data

Full-granularity SF Express panel — issuance, balances, prices, individual redemptions, and the algorithm-recommendation-vs-human-decision trace, July 2024 → present.

Approach

Normative delegation model + adherence-aware recommendation policy, with causal estimation of override value; dynamic token-economy decision model as the substrate.

Status

Active. Model formulation and identification design in progress; counterfactual policy experiments to follow.
